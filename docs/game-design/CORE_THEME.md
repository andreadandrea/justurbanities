# Core Theme — Poly Crisis & Fragmentation

> Design pillar document. This is the conceptual spine of
> *Justurbanities: Eurbanities and Fragmentation*. Narrative, mechanics
> and content should trace back to the ideas below.

## The core idea

The underlying subject of the game is the **Poly Crisis** of modern life:
the simultaneous, overlapping and mutually reinforcing crises of our time
— climate, economy, democracy, housing, care, migration, information,
trust in institutions. These do not arrive as one clear event; they
accumulate as a chronic background condition.

**Citizens rarely experience the Poly Crisis as a system.** They
experience its *subjective, everyday face*: **Fragmentation**.

```text
Poly Crisis (structural cause)  ──perceived as──▶  Fragmentation (lived experience)
```

Fragmentation is how the Poly Crisis *feels* from inside a neighbourhood:

- distance between people who should be neighbours;
- distrust of institutions and of each other;
- shared spaces emptying out;
- decisions felt as made "elsewhere", by fewer and fewer;
- exhaustion, withdrawal, the sense that things no longer hold together.

The game never lectures about the Poly Crisis directly. It lets the player
**feel the Fragmentation** at street level and then act against it.

## Why this matters for design

The response to a *structural* cause cannot be individual heroics. The
game's answer is **local, relational and collective**: people re-weaving,
in one neighbourhood, the fabric the Poly Crisis pulls apart.

This is exactly what the **collective resources** represent. They are not
"stats" the hero levels up; they are the threads of social fabric:

| Resource | What it re-weaves against Fragmentation |
|---|---|
| **Trust** | distrust between people and toward institutions |
| **Care** | exhaustion, invisible labour, people left unsupported |
| **Commons** | shared spaces and resources falling into "nobody's" |
| **Voice** | decisions made elsewhere; people unheard |
| **Resilience** | the community's capacity to absorb the next shock |

And **Fragmentation** is tracked as its own indicator — the visible
pressure of the Poly Crisis on the place.

> Resources do not "solve" the Poly Crisis. They mend the *perceived*
> fabric locally. The horizon stays open on purpose: civic action is
> ongoing, not a win condition.

## Design implications (checklist for new content)

1. **Show, don't explain.** Surface the Poly Crisis through concrete,
   ordinary frictions (a missing evening bus, a step nobody can climb, a
   noticeboard open shorter than the queue), never as an essay.
2. **Fragmentation is subjective.** Visuals/state should read as
   *disconnection and desaturation*, the felt experience — not as
   on-screen "global crisis" iconography.
3. **No lone hero.** Progress comes from connecting people and places;
   reward relational and collective acts over individual ones.
4. **Different routes, same city.** The same Poly Crisis lands unequally:
   each playable character meets different barriers (accessibility,
   language, care duties, institutional distance, being new in town).
   "Arriving is already participation."
5. **Honest endings.** The educational report mirrors *who arrived, what
   changed, what was missed* — accountability, not a victory screen.
6. **Resources are fabric, not score.** Frame gains/losses as threads
   mended or torn, never as points won.

## One-line pitch

> A civic adventure where you feel modernity's Poly Crisis as
> Fragmentation at street level, and weave it back together — with
> others — one neighbourhood relationship at a time.
